# Elements Workspace — Brand & Design System
## Complete Style Guide
### Version 1.0 · March 2026

## OVERVIEW

This document is the single source of truth for how Elements Workspace looks, sounds, and communicates across all channels — website, print, social media, email, and event materials. It exists so that anyone working on Elements communications — a designer, a guide writing a class description, a parent creating a flyer — can produce work that feels consistent and true to the organization.

The through-line across everything: **intimate scale, genuine bonds, shared purpose.**

# PART 1: BRAND IDENTITY

---

## 1.1 Who We Are

Elements Workspace is a Montessori-inspired enrichment center for homeschooling families in Brooklyn. We serve children ages 3–10 through small, consistent programs led by experienced educators who know each child specifically. We are intimate by design. Our groups are close-knit, our community is built on shared educational values, and our approach is responsive to the developmental needs and genuine interests of the children in front of us.

**We are not:** a school, a daycare, a drop-in enrichment class, or a franchise. We are a community that grows with your family.

---

## 1.2 Brand Character

Elements Workspace has a clear personality. Every piece of communication should feel like it comes from the same place.

**Confident.** We know what we are and why it works. We don't apologize for our structure, our minimums, or our philosophy. We state things plainly and let them stand.

**Warm.** We care about the families we serve. Our warmth is genuine, not performed — it comes through in specificity and honesty, not in exclamation points or over-reassurance.

**Precise.** We use exact language. We say "pre-literacy and early numeracy" not "learning basics." We say "intimate group" not "small class." We say "responsive to the developmental needs of the children in front of us" not "we meet kids where they are." The precision signals that we know what we're talking about.

**Grounded.** We don't make claims we can't support. We don't oversell. We don't address objections nobody raised. We say the true thing and stop.

---

## 1.3 Brand Positioning

**For:** Homeschooling families in Brooklyn with children ages 3–10 who have made a deliberate choice about education and are looking for a community that understands and deepens that choice.

**What we offer:** A Montessori-inspired learning environment where children are genuinely known, developmentally supported, and part of a close-knit community of families with shared purpose.

**Why it's different:** Intimate scale makes genuine responsiveness possible. Experienced guides who are also parents themselves build real knowledge of each child over time. The community that forms around shared educational values is as valuable as the programs themselves.

**The through-line:** Intimate scale. Genuine bonds. Shared purpose.

---

## 1.4 What We Are Not

These contrasts are for internal clarity — they inform how we write and position ourselves, but they never appear in public-facing copy as "we are not X."

| We are... | Not... |
|---|---|
| An enrichment center | A school |
| Montessori-inspired | Montessori-certified |
| Intimate by design | Small by accident |
| A community | A service provider |
| Responsive | Unstructured |
| Serious about learning | Academic in the conventional sense |
| Child-led within a prepared environment | Child-directed without structure |

# PART 2: VOICE & COPY

---

## 2.1 Voice Principles

**Write from the affirmative.**
Define Elements by what it is. Never apologize, never contrast, never say "unlike X." Confidence over comparison.

✓ *Elements Workspace is a Montessori-inspired enrichment center.*
✗ *Elements Workspace is not a school.*

**Say the thing. Stop.**
Once you've made a point, stop making it. Don't explain why you made it. Don't add reassurance nobody asked for. Don't address an objection that wasn't raised.

✓ *The three-day minimum is what the model requires.*
✗ *The three-day minimum is what the model requires — and we'd rather be honest about that upfront than overpromise on less.*

**Every word earns its place.**
If a sentence can be cut without losing meaning, cut it. If a word is doing no work, remove it. Copy that gets longer with each revision is copy that's losing confidence.

**Precise over vague.**
Specific language signals expertise. Vague language signals uncertainty.

✓ *Pre-literacy and early numeracy through hands-on materials*
✗ *Early learning activities*

✓ *Attunement between guides and children develops through consistency*
✗ *Your child will feel comfortable*

**State, don't sell.**
Describe what Elements is and does. Let the parent draw their own conclusions. We don't tell them what they'll feel, what they'll discover, or what they'll know pretty quickly.

✓ *Come see the space, meet the guides, bring your questions.*
✗ *Most families know pretty quickly whether this is the right fit.*

## 2.2 Tone by Context

| Context | Tone | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Homepage | Clear, warm, confident | "A community built on intimate scale, genuine bonds, and shared purpose." |
| Program descriptions | Precise, informative, direct | "Each day includes reading, writing, and mathematics — through Montessori materials, sustained project work, and guided practice." |
| FAQ | Honest, plain, no hedging | "The attunement between guides and children, the peer relationships, and the depth of work all require consistency. We've learned, through experience, that three days is where consistency becomes community." |
| Social media | Warm, specific, inviting curiosity | Surface a real moment. Ask a real question. Don't summarize the program. |
| Email to families | Conversational, direct, respectful of their time | Get to the point. Warm but not meandering. |
| Event descriptions | Specific and enticing | Name what will happen. Not "a wonderful morning" but "a morning of hands-on projects and outdoor exploration." |

---

## 2.3 Language Guide

**Always use:**
- *Enrichment center* (not school, learning center, microschool — unless specifically explaining to an unfamiliar audience)
- *Guides* (not teachers, educators — though "educator" is fine in bios and credential contexts)
- *Intimate* (not small — small undersells, intimate is precise)
- *Prepared environment* (Montessori terminology, use it — it has meaning)
- *Responsive* (to describe our approach to the child)
- *Developmental stage* (when explaining why we do what we do)
- *Homeschooling families* (not "alternative education families" — be specific)
- *Community* (not program, not service, not offering)
- *Close-knit* (when describing group dynamics)
- *Child-led / interest-based* (to describe the approach, before invoking Montessori)

**Use sparingly:**
- *Montessori* — use as a reference point for parents who need it, not as our identity or primary descriptor. Lead with what we do; Montessori is the framework name some parents will recognize.
- *Rigorous* — carries academic pressure connotations. Use *intentional* instead.
- *Unique* — overused. Show what's different; don't say it's unique.

**Never use:**
- *Unlike conventional school / traditional school* — we don't define ourselves by contrast
- *Not a school* in public copy — state what we are
- *We'd rather be honest* — implies we might not be otherwise
- *Learning* as a standalone adjective (learning home, learning space, learning journey) — the word has been drained of meaning
- *Innovative* — meaningless
- *Holistic* — vague
- *Empowering* — say what we do instead

## 2.5 What We Don't Do in Copy

- Address objections nobody raised
- Add reassurance to statements that don't need it
- End a strong statement with an explanation of why it's strong
- Use "genuinely" twice in the same section
- Explain the philosophy before naming the fact
- Use bullet points for things that read better as prose
- Put "learning" in front of nouns to make them sound educational

## 3.2 Typography

### Web Fonts (Google Fonts — both free)

**Display / Headlines: Lora**
A serif typeface with warmth and editorial quality. Use for all major headings, section titles, pull quotes, and anywhere the brand voice needs to carry weight.

- H1: Lora 400 or 500, 44–48px, line-height 1.12
- H2: Lora 400, 26–32px, line-height 1.25–1.3
- Pull quotes: Lora 400 italic, 18–22px, line-height 1.6
- Program names (cards): Lora 500, 18–20px

**Body / UI: DM Sans**
A humanist sans-serif with clarity and approachability. Use for all body text, navigation, buttons, labels, captions, and UI elements.

- Body: DM Sans 300 (light), 14–16px, line-height 1.75–1.85
- Navigation: DM Sans 400 or 500, 13px
- Buttons: DM Sans 500, 13–14px
- Labels/tags: DM Sans 500, 10–11px, letter-spacing 2–2.5px, uppercase
- Captions: DM Sans 300, 12px

### Import Code (HTML)
```html
<link href="https://fonts.googleapis.com/css2?family=Lora:ital,wght@0,400;0,500;1,400;1,500&family=DM+Sans:wght@300;400;500&display=swap" rel="stylesheet">
```

### Canva Alternatives
If Google Fonts are unavailable (print, Canva):
- Lora → Playfair Display or Cormorant Garamond
- DM Sans → Work Sans or Nunito Sans

### Typography Rules

- **Never** use more than two typefaces in a single piece
- **Lora** for emotional, brand, or philosophical content
- **DM Sans** for practical, informational, or UI content
- Body text minimum: 14px digital, 10pt print
- Line height for body: always 1.7 or above
- Letter spacing on labels/tags: 2–2.5px uppercase — no other contexts
- **Never** use bold DM Sans for body copy — use weight 500 for UI elements only

## 3.3 Spacing & Layout

### Spacing Scale
- **xs:** 8px — internal component padding
- **sm:** 12–16px — gaps between related elements
- **md:** 24–28px — gaps between cards/components
- **lg:** 40–48px — section internal padding
- **xl:** 64–72px — full section padding (top/bottom)
- **2xl:** 80px+ — hero sections

### Border Radius
- Buttons: 24–28px (pill shape)
- Cards: 12–16px
- Badges/pills: 20px
- Images: 12–16px
- Accent bars: 2px

### Grid
- Maximum content width: 1200px
- Standard page padding: 48px (desktop), 24px (mobile)
- Two-column grids: 1fr 1fr or 1.1fr 0.9fr for hero sections
- Three-column grids: 1fr 1fr 1fr for pillars, testimonials, team cards
- Four-column grids: for detail/stats strips

### Section Rhythm
Alternate section backgrounds in this order for visual rhythm:
1. Cream (#F7F3E8)
2. White (#FFFFFF)
3. Cream or accent (Sage Light, Terra Light)
4. Dark (Sage Dark, Ink)

Never use the same background for two consecutive full sections.

## 3.5 Photography & Imagery

### Style
- **Documentary, not directed.** Real moments over posed setups.
- **Natural light always.** No flash photography, no studio lighting.
- **Children at work or in genuine community.** Concentration, conversation, collaboration — not mugging for the camera.
- **The space matters.** Photos of the environment — materials, organization, light, texture — are as important as photos of children.

### What to shoot
- Children working independently with materials
- Genuine moments of focus and concentration
- Children in conversation or collaborative work
- Community moments — snack time, outdoor time, arrival
- The physical space — rooms, materials, outdoor areas
- Guide-child interactions — attentive, not performative
- Natural transitions and unhurried moments

### What to avoid
- Posed group photos
- Children looking at camera mid-activity
- Stock photography of any kind
- Overly bright or saturated editing
- Adults directing children for the camera
- Children in distress or visibly unhappy

### Editing
- Natural color grading — warm but not Instagram-filtered
- Slight lift in shadows for warmth
- Do not over-sharpen
- Consistent color temperature across all images used together

### Alt text
Every image must have descriptive alt text for accessibility:
- Describe what's happening, not what the image is for
- ✓ "A child sorting colored beads at a low wooden table, morning light through the window"
- ✗ "Elements Workspace child learning"

## 4.3 Email

**From name:** Jenny at Elements Workspace (or just Jenny for community emails)
**Email:** jenny@elementsworkspace.com

**Subject line principles:**
- Specific enough to earn the open, playful enough to invite it
- The subject line and the email should feel like the same voice
- ✓ *"Something new is coming this September — here's what we're building"*
- ✓ *"July at Elements: stories, places, and one really good guide"*
- ✓ *"Fall pricing is here. Early-bird closes July 1."*
- ✗ *"Something exciting is happening at Elements..."* — vague, earns nothing
- ✗ *"You won't want to miss this"* — generic, slightly manipulative

The test: does the subject line give the reader something real? Give them something real, but make it feel like you.

**Email structure:**
- One main point per email
- Short — if it needs more than 3 paragraphs, it needs to become two emails
- CTA in the first 3 sentences if the email has one
- No decorative sign-offs — just Jenny, or Jenny + Elements Workspace

**Tone:**
- Write like you'd talk to a parent you already know
- No corporate language
- Direct, warm, brief

# PART 5: AUDIENCE PERSONAS

---

## 5.1 Primary Audiences

**The Intentional Opt-Out**
Has researched alternative education extensively. Confident in the choice, looking for community and quality. Knows what Montessori is. Responds to: precision, philosophy, community framing. Does not need to be convinced homeschooling is valid — already there.

**The Educational Explorer**
Tried conventional school, found it wasn't right. Curious but cautious. Worried about academic gaps. Responds to: clarity about what children actually do each day (the three Rs), the three-day structure as evidence of seriousness, the guide credentials. Needs to trust before they commit.

**The Experienced Homeschooler**
Years into homeschooling. Looking for enrichment, community, and a partner who speaks their language. Responds to: the community framing, the multi-age structure, the guide-to-child knowledge. Already sold on the philosophy — needs to know Elements is the right community.

**The Brooklyn Practical**
Chose homeschooling for practical reasons. Needs convenience, reliability, community. Responds to: location, schedule clarity, the B31 transit note, the co-working space, the consistency of the program. Less interested in philosophy, more interested in fit.

---

## 5.2 Russian-Speaking Families

A meaningful portion of the Elements community comes from Russian-speaking backgrounds. Standard child-led/Montessori marketing language — which foregrounds child autonomy and can imply minimal structure — can undersell the program or raise concerns for this audience.

**Reframe for this audience:**
- Lead with the guide's expertise and credentials, not child autonomy
- Describe structure first: clear rhythms, real expectations, experienced educators
- Use "curiosity-driven choice within a prepared environment" rather than "children direct their own learning"
- Academics (reading, writing, mathematics) should appear early in descriptions
- The guide-as-parent detail resonates strongly — emphasize it

**Language note:** Elements programs are conducted in English. Some guides have Russian language ability, but instruction is English-language. This should be communicated clearly upfront to set the right expectations.

**Channels:** Word of mouth is the primary channel for this community. Telegram group communications, Facebook groups, community events, and personal referrals from existing families are more effective than cold marketing.
